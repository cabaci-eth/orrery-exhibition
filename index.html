A Mechanism for Seeing Time.

01 — The instrument

Five gears, one year per turn.

A central fifty-tooth drive gear carries the Sun. Each planet gear is cut with the exact tooth count that makes it spin at that planet's real orbital frequency. Crank the Sun, drag it, or let it run.

02 — True scale

The same four worlds, at their real relative sizes.

The gears show time. Here is size — the four model balls at one millimetre to 637 kilometres. Venus and Earth come out as near-perfect twins.

Mercury is a marble; Mars a little larger; Venus and Earth are twins, separated here by less than a millimetre.

At this same scale the Sun would be 2.18 metres across — far too large to place beside these, which is why on the board it stands in symbolically at the centre.
03 — The physics

Two laws, meshed together.

A planet's year is fixed by how far it orbits; a gear's speed is fixed by how many teeth it has. Choose the teeth correctly and one law impersonates the other.

Kepler's Third Law

The square of a planet's orbital period T is proportional to the cube of its mean distance a from the Sun. Mercury sits close, so its year is short and it laps the others again and again.

The gear-meshing law

Two meshed gears turn at a ratio set only by their tooth counts N. Drive a planet gear from the fifty-tooth Sun and it spins 50 ÷ N times per year — chosen to match that planet's real orbital frequency to under one percent.
